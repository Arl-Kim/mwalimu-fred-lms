Determining Boiling Points of Pure and Impure Substances


Requirements for the Experiment
  • Boiling tube
  • Thermometer
  • Heating apparatus
  • Distilled water
  • A spatula
  • Salt and water

Procedure for the Experiment Above
  • Put about 10cm3 of distilled water in a boiling tube.
  • Close the tube with a stopper that has two holes.
  • Pass a thermometer through one hole and immerse the thermometer bulb into the water.
  • Push an ‘L’ shaped tube through the other hole.
  • Heat the apparatus using a small flame.
  • Observe the changes in temperature and record your observations.
  • Cool the apparatus and remove the stopper.
  • Add a spoonful of salt to some water and stir to dissolve all the salt to form a salty water solution.
  • Repeat the experiment using the salty water solution in place of pure distilled water.
  • Observe changes in temperature and record your observations. What conclusion have you made?
  • At what temperature does pure water boil?
  • At what temperature does water with dissolved salt (impure water) boil?
  • Compare your observations.

Observations, Conclusion & Explanation
  • When distilled water is heated, the temperature of the water rises to about 100°C.
  • After this, the temperature remains constant for some time. The heat absorbed changes liquid water into water vapor or steam.
  • Pure water has a definite or specific boiling point.
  • Salty (impure) water has a range of boiling temperatures above 100°C.
  • Impurities cause a rise in the boiling point of liquids.
  • The greater the impurities in a given solution, the higher the boiling point. Therefore, we can use the boiling point to determine the purity of a liquid.
